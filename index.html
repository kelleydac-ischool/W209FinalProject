Navigating the 2013 California STAR standardized testing results
for San Francisco

Created by Jess Hinel and Doug Kelley for UCB MIDS W209 Summer 2015

The California Department of Education provides an anonymized summary of the STAR standardized test results broken down by school, test, grade, and demographic groups, but sorting through the data to answer some simple questions can be challenging. We have created this portal to simplify the process and make the data more accessible.

The visualizations below present the CalSTAR for San Francisco in 2013 to try to answer 6 questions:

  1. Do charter schools play a significant role in Sanfrancisco? Where are they located in the city?

  2. How is performance on the CalSTAR tests distributed about the city for different grades?

  3. Is there a performance difference if students take a particular test in different grades?

  4. Are there schools where students perform well on several tests?

  5. Can we create a profile to compare school performance for a given test and grade?

  6. Is there a pattern of performance across demographic groups within a school?

Do charter schools show a different pattern of performance from traditional public schools in San Francisco?

Charter schools have been a novel but controversial addition to the public education system over the past two decades. While some see them as a way to improve student performance by allowing greater autonomy and providing more accountability for the school administration and faculty, others question whether these changes really result in any educational gains, and question the role and motives of private companies in operating these charters.

How do we characterize performance?

Performance is broken down into five levels:

  • Advanced
  • Proficient
  • Basic
  • Below Basic
  • Far Below Basic
For simplicity, we show only the top four levels below.

Each test can be taken in different grades depending on the classes in which the student is enrolled. The dataset is anonymized in that no group of fewer than 10 students is represented in the dataset. The subgroup data only includes the percentage at or above proficient, not the full breakdown into five levels, to keep the data anonymous.

More information on the scoring methods and the performance standards can be found here.

The visualizations below will show the following points:

  • Charters are concentrated in the central and southeastern parts of San Francisco.
  • Higher scores are typically found in the northern and western parts of San Francisco, and lower scores in the central and southeastern parts.
  • Schools tend to have good or poor performance across several subjects and demographic groups.
  • However, tests for some subjects (primarily math but not science) taken in later grades show poorer performance than when taken in earlier grades for all schools.

We find charter schools in the central and southeastern parts of San Francisco.

While not all schools report data from all tests, we can compare performance from several tests (like CST Science) wich are reported from many zip codes<./h3>

For many tests, we find better performance in the western part of the city and poorer performance in the southeastern part of the city.

We find several schools with high performance in several subjects across several grades, and other schools with poorer performance across subjects and across grades.

When we compare the performance profile of several schools, we only find traditional schools with left-weighted performance profiles.

When we look across demographic groups, we find the same performance clusters for several groups.

We find that students who take some math class in lower grades show better performance than those who take the test in later grades, an exception to the clustering we saw earlier.

Some schools, particularly in the western part of the city, show very high performance in a number of subjects among several demographic groups. One exception for some subjects like geometry is that students who take this test in later grades typically show lower performance levels. While this pattern holds for math tests, it does not appear to hold for science tests.

Schools in the central and southeastern parts of the city typically show lower performance levels. Charters cluster in these lower performing areas, and show equivalent performance to traditional public schools. This dataset does not show a significant performance difference for charters beyond the location effect (which may be primarily reflective of socioeconomic factors).

Do these charts tell us which schools are better than others? As discussed at length by Diane Ravitch, standardized testing performance may be a very poor indicator of educational quality, and may have far more to do with the familiarity of the students with the test procedures than the material itself. Further, the performance standards are not fixed and are often adjusted from year to year.
